Zap is most powerful when it is presented as a clear position-management action, not as a raw program interaction. The user should understand the outcome: enter, exit, claim and convert, or rebalance.

## Start With User Intent

Every Zap flow should begin with a simple user question:

- "What token do you have?"
- "What position do you want?"
- "What token do you want to end with?"
- "How much do you want to convert?"
- "What range or strategy should your liquidity use?"

Once the intent is clear, the frontend or integrator can choose the right Zap flow.

## Design a Zap In Experience

A good Zap In experience hides unnecessary mechanics while showing the decisions that matter.

Show users:

- The pool or position they are entering.
- The starting token and input amount.
- The expected swap or swaps needed before deposit.
- The expected token mix used for liquidity.
- The position type and range.
- Slippage or price-change settings.
- The final position outcome.

Avoid making users reason about temporary ledger mechanics. The ledger is useful infrastructure, not the product story.

## Design a Zap Out Experience

Zap Out is best shown as an action attached to another position operation.

Examples:

- "Remove liquidity and convert to USDC"
- "Claim fees and convert to SOL"
- "Claim rewards and swap 50%"
- "Exit position to one token"

Show users:

- The token expected from the preceding action.
- The token they want to receive.
- The percentage being converted.
- The max amount that can be swapped.
- The route provider: DAMM v2, DLMM, or Jupiter.
- Estimated output and price impact where available.

<Info>
  Zap Out is percentage-based on the newly received amount in the flow. This makes it useful for claim-and-convert experiences because it does not need to swap the user's full wallet balance.
</Info>

## Design a DLMM Rebalance Experience

DLMM rebalancing is one of the strongest Zap use cases because position maintenance can be tedious.

A rebalance experience should show:

- Current position range.
- Current active bin or market area.
- Proposed new range.
- Strategy: Spot, Curve, or Bid-Ask.
- Expected token mix after withdrawal and swaps.
- Slippage settings.
- Any remaining token amounts after redeposit.

For users, the story is simple: move liquidity from where it is no longer useful to where trading is happening now.

## Route Selection Guidance

Zap supports direct Meteora routes and Jupiter routes for Zap Out.

| Route Choice | Use When |
| --- | --- |
| DAMM v2 | The desired swap can route efficiently through a DAMM v2 pool. |
| DLMM | The desired swap can route efficiently through a DLMM pool. |
| Jupiter v6 | The best available path may require aggregator routing. |
| Jupiter v6 shared-account route | The frontend or route builder specifically uses Jupiter's supported shared-account route. |

For most user experiences, route selection should be quote-driven. Show the route when it matters, but lead with the user's final outcome.

## Recommended UX Patterns

### Add With One Token

Best for users who do not want to manually acquire both sides of a pool.

```text
Input token: SOL
Target: SOL-USDC liquidity position
Flow: swap part of SOL to USDC, then deposit both sides
```

### Claim and Convert

Best for users who earn fees or rewards in a token they do not want to hold.

```text
Claim: position fees
Convert: 100% of newly claimed token to USDC
Flow: claim, detect received amount, swap through supported route
```

### Exit to Preferred Token

Best for users closing or reducing a position.

```text
Remove liquidity
Convert one or both output tokens
End with user's preferred token
```

### Recenter DLMM Position

Best for active DLMM LPs.

```text
Withdraw old range
Prepare token mix
Deposit into new range around active bin
```

## Communicate the Important Risks

Zap reduces workflow complexity, but it cannot remove market mechanics.

Users should understand:

- Swap quotes can change before execution.
- AMM price can move during the flow.
- Slippage settings can cause the flow to fail or execute with a wider range of outcomes.
- Token 2022 behavior may affect net received amounts.
- Some routes or tokens may not be supported.
- Remaining dust can happen when token amounts do not perfectly match deposit requirements.

<Warning>
  Product copy should not imply that Zap always gives the "best" output. Zap can coordinate supported routes, but route quality depends on liquidity, quotes, slippage, and market movement.
</Warning>

## Pre-Launch Checklist for Integrators

Before exposing a Zap flow, confirm:

- The target product is supported: DAMM v2 or DLMM.
- The target route is supported for Zap Out.
- The token mints work with the underlying pool or route.
- The frontend has quote data for any required swaps.
- Slippage settings are visible and reasonable.
- The user can preview the expected final position or output token.
- The flow handles remaining token amounts or dust gracefully.
- The flow explains what happens if a route fails.
- The temporary ledger lifecycle is handled cleanly for Zap In flows.

## Good Product Copy

Use outcome-first language:

- "Add liquidity with SOL"
- "Exit to USDC"
- "Claim and convert"
- "Rebalance around current price"
- "Move liquidity back in range"

Avoid implementation-first language:

- "Initialize ledger account"
- "Update post-swap delta balance"
- "Modify payload amount offset"
- "Invoke remaining accounts"

Those details belong in developer documentation. Product documentation should help users understand why the flow exists and when to use it.
